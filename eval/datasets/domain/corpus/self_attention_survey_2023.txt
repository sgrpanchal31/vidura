A Survey of Large Language Models
Zhao, Zhou, Li, Tang, Wang, Hou, Min, Zhang, Zhang, Dong, Du, Yang, Chen, Chen, Jiang, Ren, Li, Tang, Liu, Liu, Nie, Wen (2023)

Since the introduction of Transformer, language models have rapidly scaled in size, capability, and impact. This survey provides a comprehensive overview of large language models (LLMs), covering their technical foundations, training approaches, capabilities, safety considerations, and applications.

Key milestones in LLM development:
- 2017: Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al.)
- 2018: BERT (bidirectional pretraining), GPT (autoregressive pretraining)
- 2019: GPT-2, T5, RoBERTa, XLNet
- 2020: GPT-3 (175B, few-shot learning), scaling laws (Kaplan et al.)
- 2021: FLAN (instruction tuning), Codex, Switch Transformer
- 2022: InstructGPT (RLHF), PaLM, Chinchilla, Chain-of-Thought
- 2023: LLaMA, GPT-4, Claude 2, Llama 2, Mistral

Emergent abilities observed in LLMs (abilities not present in smaller models):
- In-context learning (GPT-3 scale, ~100B+ parameters)
- Chain-of-thought reasoning (~100B+ parameters)
- Instruction following (~100B+ parameters)
- Code generation

Key training techniques:
- Pretraining: autoregressive LM or masked LM on large text corpora
- Instruction tuning: supervised fine-tuning on instruction-response pairs
- RLHF: reward modeling + PPO optimization on human preference data
- Constitutional AI: AI-generated feedback for harmlessness training

Alignment challenges: Current LLMs exhibit sycophancy, hallucination, and various biases. Active areas of research include RLHF improvements, Constitutional AI, debate, and scalable oversight.

LLMs are increasingly deployed as the backbone of AI agents that use tools, maintain memory, and operate in multi-step loops. RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) has emerged as the dominant approach for grounding LLMs in factual external knowledge.
