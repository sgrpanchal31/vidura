A Simple Framework for Contrastive Learning of Visual Representations (SimCLR)
Chen, Kornblith, Norouzi, Hinton (2020)

This paper presents SimCLR, a simple framework for contrastive self-supervised learning of visual representations. SimCLR learns representations by maximizing agreement between differently augmented views of the same data example.

The framework:
1. Apply two different random data augmentations to the same image, creating two views (x_i, x_j)
2. Encode both views with a shared encoder network f (ResNet)
3. Project to a lower-dimensional space with a small MLP head g
4. Apply NT-Xent (normalized temperature-scaled cross entropy) loss to pull paired views together and push unpaired views apart

Key findings from ablation studies:
- Composition of multiple augmentations is critical: random cropping + color distortion dramatically outperforms individual augmentations
- A nonlinear projection head g improves representation quality by 3-10% vs linear projection
- Larger batch sizes (4096-8192) and longer training (800 epochs) consistently improve performance
- Temperature τ=0.1 works best for NT-Xent loss

Results on ImageNet linear evaluation (ResNet-50):
- SimCLR: 76.5% top-1 accuracy with 100 epochs of pretraining
- SimCLR: 69.3% top-1 with just 10 epochs of pretraining

With 1% ImageNet labels (semi-supervised):
- SimCLR achieves 85.8% top-5 accuracy (vs supervised baseline 80.4%)

SimCLR closes 70% of the gap between previous self-supervised methods and supervised ResNet-50, establishing contrastive learning as a practical path to learning representations without labels.
