Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks
Lewis, Perez, Piktus, Petroni, Karpukhin, Goyal, Küttler, Lewis, Yih, Rocktäschel, Riedel, Kiela (2020)

ABSTRACT

Large pre-trained language models have been shown to store factual knowledge in their parameters, and achieve state-of-the-art results when fine-tuned on downstream NLP tasks. However, their ability to access and precisely manipulate knowledge is still limited, and hence on knowledge-intensive tasks, their performance lags behind task-specific architectures. Additionally, providing provenance for their decisions and updating their world knowledge remain open research problems. We explore a general-purpose fine-tuning recipe for retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) -- models which combine pre-trained parametric and non-parametric memory for language generation.

ARCHITECTURE

RAG combines two components:

1. Retriever: Uses Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR) to retrieve relevant documents from Wikipedia. DPR uses a bi-encoder architecture: a query encoder and a document encoder, both based on BERT-base. The retriever finds the top-k documents (typically k=5 or k=10) from a Wikipedia dump of 21 million passages, each a 100-word chunk.

2. Generator: Uses BART-large (400M parameters) as the sequence-to-sequence generator. BART takes the concatenation of the input query and each retrieved document as input, and generates the final answer.

Two variants are proposed:
- RAG-Sequence: Uses the same retrieved document to generate the entire sequence.
- RAG-Token: Can use different documents at each generation step, allowing different parts of the answer to come from different documents.

RETRIEVAL MECHANISM

The retriever uses Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS) over document embeddings pre-computed with the DPR document encoder. The Wikipedia corpus is encoded offline, and at inference time, only the query needs to be encoded. The top-k most similar passages are retrieved using FAISS for efficient approximate nearest neighbor search.

TRAINING

RAG is trained end-to-end, with the query encoder and the BART generator fine-tuned jointly using the marginal log-likelihood of generating the correct answer, marginalized over the retrieved documents. The document encoder is kept frozen during training.

RESULTS

Open-domain Question Answering:

Natural Questions:
- RAG-Token: 44.5 Exact Match (vs BART closed-book: 26.5, T5 closed-book: 36.6)
- RAG-Sequence: 45.5 Exact Match

TriviaQA:
- RAG-Token: 68.0 Exact Match (outperforms all previous open-domain QA methods)
- RAG-Sequence: 68.0 Exact Match

WebQuestions:
- RAG-Token: 45.5 Exact Match
- RAG-Sequence: 45.2 Exact Match

FEVER (Fact Verification):
- RAG achieves 84.8% accuracy, outperforming previous non-retrieval models

Jeopardy Question Generation:
- RAG generates more factually specific and human-preferred outputs compared to BART and T5

Knowledge-Grounded Dialogue (Wizard of Wikipedia):
- RAG achieves lower perplexity than BART

ADVANTAGES OVER PARAMETRIC-ONLY MODELS

1. Knowledge updating: The retriever's document store can be updated without retraining the model.
2. Provenance: Retrieved documents can be surfaced to users as citations.
3. Factual accuracy: RAG hallucinates less than pure parametric models.
4. Specialization: The document store can be swapped to specialize RAG for specific domains.

CONCLUSION

RAG outperforms both parametric-only models (BART, T5) and retrieval-only models on open-domain QA tasks. The combination of parametric and non-parametric memory allows RAG to perform accurate and verifiable generation, establishing a new paradigm for knowledge-intensive NLP.
