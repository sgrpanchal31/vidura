Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models (Chinchilla)
Hoffmann, Borgeaud, Mensch, Buchatskia, Cai, Rutherford, Casanova, Clark, Hendricks, Welbl, Clark, Hennigan, Noland, Davison, Jeblick, Driessche, Crome, Guy, Osindero, Kavukcuoglu, Hassabis, Rae, Sifre, Sifre (2022)

We investigate the optimal model size and number of training tokens for a given compute budget. We find that current large language models are significantly undertrained relative to the compute-optimal frontier.

By training over 400 language models ranging from 70M to over 16B parameters on 5 to 500 billion tokens, we find that for compute-optimal training, the model size and the number of training tokens should be scaled equally — for every doubling of model size, training tokens should also double.

This directly contradicts the Kaplan et al. (2020) scaling laws, which suggested larger models are preferable given a fixed compute budget.

Chinchilla scaling law: for a compute budget C (measured in FLOPs), the optimal model size N and token count D satisfy:
N_opt ∝ C^{0.5}
D_opt ∝ C^{0.5}

To validate this, we train Chinchilla — a 70 billion parameter model trained on 1.4 trillion tokens — and compare it to Gopher (280B on 300B tokens).

Results:
- Chinchilla (70B, 1.4T tokens) outperforms Gopher (280B, 300B tokens) despite 4× fewer parameters
- Chinchilla achieves 67.5 on MMLU (vs Gopher 60.0, GPT-3 43.9)
- Chinchilla achieves 79.9% accuracy on BIG-Bench Language Understanding (vs Gopher 69.7)
- HellaSwag: 80.8% (vs Gopher 79.2%)
- Lower training and inference cost than Gopher

Chinchilla's findings shifted the field: subsequent models including LLaMA, Mistral, and Llama 2 adopted the principle of training smaller models on far more tokens.
