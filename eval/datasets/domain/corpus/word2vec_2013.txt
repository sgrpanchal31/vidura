Distributed Representations of Words and Phrases and their Compositionality
Mikolov, Sutskever, Chen, Corrado, Dean (2013)

The recently introduced continuous Skip-gram model is an efficient method for learning high-quality distributed vector representations that capture a large number of precise syntactic and semantic word relationships. In this paper we present several extensions that improve both the quality of the vectors and the training speed.

The Skip-gram model learns vector representations by predicting surrounding words given a center word. The model is trained by maximizing the average log probability of surrounding words within a context window.

Key improvements introduced:
- Negative sampling: Replace the softmax over the full vocabulary with a binary classification task over a small number of negative samples (5-20). This dramatically reduces training time.
- Subsampling of frequent words: Randomly discard frequent words like "the" and "a" during training. Words with frequency f are discarded with probability sqrt(t/f) where t ≈ 10⁻⁵.
- Phrase detection: Identify common phrases (e.g., "New York") and treat them as single tokens.

Results on the word analogy task (king - man + woman ≈ queen):
- 72% accuracy on syntactic analogies
- 55% accuracy on semantic analogies

The learned representations capture surprisingly rich semantic relationships. Vector arithmetic produces meaningful results: vec("Russia") + vec("river") ≈ vec("Volga River").

word2vec established distributed word representations as a foundation for NLP and directly influenced the development of subsequent embedding models including GloVe, FastText, and transformer-based embeddings.
